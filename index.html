Conducted in May 2023

ORIGINAL DESIGN

Background

My case study focuses on the re-design for a government agency webpage: The Department of Interiors of the United States. The project was conducted independently.

Challenges

At first glance, users weren't confident in the webpages purpose, two out of five mentioned thinking it was for a travel or vacation site. Through user research, it was determined that the navigation is not intuitive for the majority of users.

.

These issues made it less likely for users to engadge in a meaningful way.

Solution

Clarify the purpose and redesign the navigation to be more intuative for users. Read onwards to see my solutions and design decisions.

How do users interact with the Original website design....?

For the original webpage I conducted a moderated, remote, usability study. Between May 10th and May 11th, 2023,  Five participants complete three user tasks on the Government of Interior webpage.  A peer, assisted me in  conducting usability tests and data collection.

Synthasizing Data

How?

Priority Matrix-User Paths-User journey mapping-Empathy Mapping Online Survey

The original design had a large hero image which gave the impression upon some users that it was travel related. It is a simple and clean design with little content above the fold. Although visually appealing, it increases the likelyhood that users need to spend mental energy finding what they are looking for.

Insights....

100% of people experienced confusion when navigating the original site.

"I'm not sure I would click on Careers and Media (to reach the volunteer page)"

anonymous participant from usability study.

Most users don't know what the site is for upon initial glance.

"What am I looking at? A travel site?"

anonymous participant from usability study.

Time to get to work!

Next was designing the low-fidelity prototypes for the new design. My attention was on: navigation making sense to the users by changing terminologies that don't resignate with them. Ensure the purpose is more obvious.

So, did I get it right....?

The only way to know is to put my design sketches to the test

5 users participated in a usability study between May 10th-11th, 2023.

I used 5 second testing, because this would allow me to capture initial impact and imppression from users.

I asked users to compelete 4 tasks without my assistance

Oh no,

I had changed the original term "join" to "careers", but that did NOT resignate with most users.

60% of users did not understand the new terminology! For example:

"Oh, I don't think I would classify volunteering under careers. When I think of the word "CAREER" I think of a paid job.

anonymous participant from usability study.
Oh, Phew!

My participants were asked to complete the same four tasks that had been used in the testing for the original design. In the re-design users could complete all tasks successfully!

The Impact:

That's a 20% increase in success rate.
The Results....

I added a welcome banner, with components that span past the screen. This prompts users to scroll horizontally allowing for additional content to be seen and ensures the purpose of the webpage is clear.

I have also included "Most Popular Services". I ensured it remained above the fold so users have a greater understanding of the services provided.

So, What's next...?

Clean up the Hi-Fi prototypes for both desktop and mobile (ensure all components are interacting correctly) 

Develop the Hi-Fi mobile menu for iterated prototype 

Change ‘Careers & Media’ to ‘Join Us & Media’ consistently in prototype components. Currently, some component still have the old terminology

To further intuitive navigation and information hierarchy, I would next test my assumption that: ‘Join Us & Media’ could be changed by having the  ‘Media’  portion separate from the ‘Join Us’. Further testing is required. A/B Testing on hero carousel images
